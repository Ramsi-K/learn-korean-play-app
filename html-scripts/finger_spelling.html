About American Sign Language

About ASL
Deaf Culture
Learning Tips

American Sign Language (ASL) is a complete, natural language that has the same linguistic properties as spoken languages, with grammar that differs from English.

ASL is expressed by movements of the hands and face. It is the primary language of many North Americans who are deaf and hard of hearing and is used by some hearing people as well.

Key facts about ASL:

  • ASL is a distinct language with its own grammar and syntax
  • It's not a signed version of English
  • Facial expressions and body movements are important grammatical markers
  • ASL has regional variations and dialects
  • There are many different sign languages around the world

Deaf culture is the set of social beliefs, behaviors, art, literary traditions, history, values, and shared institutions of communities that are influenced by deafness and which use sign languages as the main means of communication.

Important aspects of Deaf culture:

  • Identity: Many Deaf people consider themselves part of a cultural and linguistic minority rather than disabled
  • Community: Strong sense of community and shared experiences
  • Values: Eye contact and visual communication are highly valued
  • Art: Rich tradition of storytelling, poetry, and theater
  • History: Important events and figures in Deaf history

Learning ASL is a rewarding experience that opens doors to new friendships and cultural understanding. Here are some tips for success:

  • Practice regularly: Consistency is key when learning any new language
  • Use facial expressions: ASL relies heavily on facial grammar
  • Focus on meaning: Don't try to translate word-for-word from English
  • Watch signers: Observe fluent signers as much as possible
  • Join the community: Attend Deaf events when possible
  • Be patient: Language learning takes time and effort
  • Have fun: Enjoy the process of learning!
